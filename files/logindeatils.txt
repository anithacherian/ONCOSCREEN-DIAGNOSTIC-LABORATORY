A Complete Blood Count (CBC) is one of the most common and essential blood tests used in modern medicine to evaluate your overall health. Think of it as a "health snapshot" that counts and examines the three main types of cells in your blood: red cells, white cells, and platelets

Risk Factors & Symptoms (When to get tested)
Persistent Fatigue: Often linked to low Hemoglobin or Iron.
Frequent Infections: May indicate a low WBC count.
Slow Healing: If cuts don't stop bleeding, your Platelet count may be low.
Unexplained Weight Loss: Can be a signal for more serious underlying blood conditions.
2. Age-Specific Recommendations

Teenagers/Young Adults: 
Screened for lifestyle-induced anemia or "Mono" (viral infections).
Seniors (60+): 
Monitored for bone marrow health and chronic inflammatory markers.
3. Gender-Specific Considerations
Females: Usually have lower baseline Hemoglobin than males due to menstrual cycles; regular checks are vital to prevent Iron Deficiency Anemia.
Males: Higher Hemoglobin levels are normal, but excessively high levels may indicate heart or smoking-related issues.

A Lipid Profile (or lipid panel) is a comprehensive blood test that measures various fat molecules (lipids) in the blood to assess cardiovascular health and the risk of heart disease or stroke. 

Lifestyle Factors: A diet high in saturated/trans fats, lack of regular physical activity, and tobacco smoking (which lowers "good" HDL cholesterol).
Obesity: Having a Body Mass Index (BMI) of 30 or higher significantly increases the likelihood of high LDL ("bad" cholesterol).
Existing Health Conditions: Individuals with Type 2 Diabetes, high blood pressure (hypertension), or chronic kidney disease are at much higher risk for lipid imbalances.

Universal Screening: All children should have their first lipid profile done once between the ages of 9 and 11.
Second Check: A second universal screening should occur between 17 and 21.
Early Risk: Children with a family history of early heart disease should be tested as early as age 2.
Young Adults (Ages 20‚Äì39)
Baseline: If you haven't been tested as a teen, get a baseline profile at age 20.
Frequency: If results are normal and there are no new risk factors, you should repeat the test every 4 to 6 years.
Middle-Aged Adults (Ages 40‚Äì65)
Routine: This is the highest risk period for plaque buildup in arteries. Testing is recommended annually or every 2 years.
Risk Calculation: Doctors use these results to calculate your 10-year cardiovascular risk score.
Seniors (Ages 65+ )
Annual Checks: Regular monitoring is essential to manage medication (like statins) and prevent late-life cardiac events.

Breast Cancer Screening is a preventive health process used to detect early signs of cancer in the breast tissue before any physical symptoms, such as lumps, appear. Early detection is critical as it significantly increases the success rate of treatment and long-term survival.

Mammography (Gold Standard): A low-dose X-ray that can identify tumours too small to be felt.
Breast Ultrasound (Sono-mammography): Uses sound waves to check if a lump is a solid mass or a fluid-filled cyst; often used for women with dense breast tissue.
Breast MRI: A highly sensitive imaging test using magnets, typically reserved for high-risk individuals or to supplement mammograms.

Genetic Mutations: The most significant risk is inherited mutations in the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes. Women with these mutations have a significantly higher lifetime risk.
Family History: Your risk increases if a first-degree relative (mother, sister, or daughter) has had breast or ovarian cancer.
Breast Density: Women with dense breast tissue (more glandular/fibrous tissue than fatty tissue) have a higher risk, and it also makes mammograms harder to read.

Ages 20 ‚Äì 39: Awareness Phase
Self-Exams: Women should be "breast aware." If you notice a change in shape, skin texture, or a lump, see a doctor immediately.
Clinical Exam: A physical exam by a doctor every 1‚Äì3 years is recommended.
High-Risk Exception: If you have a known genetic mutation (BRCA), screening with Breast MRI often begins at age 25‚Äì30.
Ages 40 ‚Äì 49: The Decision Window
Choice: Many guidelines state women should have the choice to start annual mammograms at age 40.
New Standards: Most modern medical groups now strongly recommend starting every other year at age 40 to catch aggressive cancers early.
Ages 50 ‚Äì 74: The Critical Phase
Mammography: This is the peak age for risk. A mammogram is recommended every 1 to 2 years.
Consistency: Missing a cycle increases the chance of a cancer being found at a later, harder-to-treat stage.
Ages 75+: The Individual Phase
Evaluation: Screening usually continues as long as a woman is in good health and has a life expectancy of 10 years or more.
Stopping: If the risks of the screening process (and potential follow-up surgeries) outweigh the benefits, your doctor may suggest stopping.
